The APPROTECT glitch — what it bought you

The nRF52840 has a hardware APPROTECT bit that blocks SWD read access to firmware. Production SP-1 units ship with it enabled. The chip's silicon revision is vulnerable to a known voltage-glitch technique (published by limitedresults in 2020): drop the supply rail for a few nanoseconds at exactly the right moment during boot and the protection check "fails open," enabling SWD even though APPROTECT is still set.

murray automated this with a BeagleBone Black, using its PRU real-time units for 5 ns pulse precision, a MOSFET to short the DEC1 decoupling cap, and a J-Link retrying after each attempt. Success rate was roughly 1-in-thousands, so elew joined with 5 more units to run attempts in parallel. On 2025-01-25, attempt #8,504 connected, and the full ~256 KB nRF flash (bootloader + app) was dumped.

◆ The dump is the foundation — but you don't need to repeat it

That single dump fueled the Ghidra disassembly, TimK's clean-room eMMC driver, and ericlewis's audiothingies audio-engine reimplementation. For your own firmware you do not care about APPROTECT at all — the Track 1+4 bootloader lets you flash the app slot directly, and APPROTECT is irrelevant to that workflow.

The cleverest extraction: LED bit-banging

You don't need 8,504 glitch attempts to read a device's firmware. zee_33 showed that because the Track 1+4 bootloader lets you flash any firmware, you can flash a tiny (~4 KB) program that reads flash bytes and encodes them as patterns on the 8 LEDs, film it with a phone camera, and decode the video with OpenCV.

It works because APPROTECT only blocks external SWD reads — code running on the chip can read all of flash freely, including the untouched bootloader region. It's slow but needs no exotic hardware and doesn't even require opening the case. The security lesson: "firmware is read-protected" and "the device runs only trusted code" are different claims, and APPROTECT only addresses the first.

✕ Don't conflate TimK and ericlewis
