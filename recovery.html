Debug & recovery

How you'll find bugs and how you'll get out of trouble. Two realities shape everything here: there's no factory debug header, and there's no reset pin. So you either commit to soldering SWD, or you get good at debugging through the serial console — and either way you keep a recovery path ready before you flash.

Debugging without a debugger

Most people develop without SWD at first. It's slower (no breakpoints) but entirely workable if you lean on four things:

CDC serial console
Build with -DSNIPPET=cdc-acm-console; the device exposes a serial port and a stem> shell. Sprinkle LOG_INF() / printk() and watch with screen / picocom.
A status line
The tape looper prints one line per loop — sample rate, song, transport, battery, BPM, per-track state, and crucially ovr=0 rerr=0 werr=0 health counters. A live status line is the cheapest "is it healthy?" signal you can build.
ResetBreadcrumbs
The BSP records why the last reset happened (normal / watchdog / panic) in persistent storage. After a crash-reboot, read it back — this is how you debug a device that resets faster than you can read logs.
The watchdog
A fed watchdog turns a hang into a reboot you can diagnose, instead of a dead device. Register a channel, feed() it in your loop.
✕ The console's blind spot

The CDC console only exists after the app boots far enough to bring up USB. If your firmware crashes before that, you see nothing — that's exactly the case SWD exists for. A clean reboot-on-fault handler (turn any fatal fault into sys_reboot(), as the looper does) at least keeps a pre-USB crash from looking like a brick.

Adding an SWD header (the real-development path)

The community consensus is blunt: "you can't properly develop firmware for this without fully opening the device and adding the SWD header." TimK did exactly that. You need SWDIO, SWCLK, GND, and VDD (or a matched 3.3 V), and a probe — J-Link, a DAPLink board, Black Magic Probe, or an nRF52 DK reflashed as a probe. Then ninja -C build flash works directly and you get breakpoints.

◆ Pad locations: check the wiki first

As of the skill's synthesis date the exact SWDIO/SWCLK pad locations were not yet publicly documented — TimK said he'd add them to github.com/timknapen/SP-1-dev/wiki. Check there (and the test-points page) before opening the device; if it's still missing, ask in Discord rather than probing blind.

✕ APPROTECT still applies

A factory chip has APPROTECT set, so SWD will not read the original firmware — only mass-erase + reprogram (which wipes stock firmware). That's fine for developing your own firmware. Reading the original out needs the glitch attack from chapter 02.

Opening the device safely

You only need to open it for SWD, eMMC test points, or mods. The case is glued, not screwed.

  • Heat, don't force. The white plastic piece around the play/function buttons is glued in one spot. Warm it with a hairdryer (not a hot-air gun — too hot melts plastic), then ease it with a plastic spudger over several heat-and-pry cycles. Brute force bends it permanently.
  • The FPC is the fragile part. The 19-pin, 0.3 mm-pitch Omron flex connects the button/LED face to the main board. Lift the ZIF lever, slide the ribbon out (never pull straight up), reverse to reseat.
  • Right-angle, low-profile SWD header so the case still closes.
  • Reassembly: the original adhesive usually re-sticks; if not, a little VHB tape — never superglue.
▲ The single most common "brick" is not a brick

After reassembly, ~90% of "it won't turn on" cases are an FPC that looks seated but isn't. Symptoms: powers on but no buttons/LEDs respond. Reseat the ribbon before assuming hardware failure. The Lines thread's canonical example: "just bricked one… scratch that, I just fixed it, guess the ribbon cable was slightly bent." Lines #118

Recovery checklist (device won't boot)

  1. Reseat the FPC. (Solves most cases.)
  2. Check the battery connector — the small plug works loose.
  3. Plug in USB-C and confirm a charge indication (proves the USB power path).
  4. Try Track 1 + Track 4 + USB-C — if it enumerates as a serial/CDC device, the chip is alive and you can reflash.
  5. Still dead? Reopen and inspect for trace damage near the FPC.

If a trace is damaged, repair is possible with conductive ink or fine bond wire, but most people stop here. The best insurance remains the one from the lifecycle page: never flash without a working route back to the bootloader already in your firmware.
