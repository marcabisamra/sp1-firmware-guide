Teenage Engineering · nRF52840 · Zephyr

Build your own firmware for the SP‑1

A guided path from "I have a sealed Kanye × TE stem player on my desk" to "I am compiling and flashing custom firmware I wrote." Distilled from three years of community reverse-engineering — and written to match the primary sources, not to guess.

What this is. The SP-1 (a.k.a. Stem Player SP-01) is an unreleased prototype built around a Nordic nRF52840 SoC with a 4 GB eMMC. The community bypassed its security, dumped the firmware, documented a bootloader you can reach with two buttons, and published a buildable Zephyr board support package. That means you can write C++, compile it, and flash it over USB-C with no soldering.

What you'll be able to do by the end. Understand the hardware, reach the bootloader and speak its protocol, stand up the Zephyr toolchain, build the public BSP, understand the audio engine and on-disk format well enough to play your own stems, and ship a first firmware safely.

▲ The one rule that matters most

This device has a community that has publicly called out AI hallucinations about it — by name, with corrections. Wrong opcodes, a fictional "24-bit little-endian PCM" format, invented workflow steps. This guide cites a source for every technical claim and flags the known traps. When you extend it with an AI assistant, hold it to the same standard. See Don't hallucinate.

The journey, in eight moves

Know the machine

One Cortex-M4F, two audio codecs, an eMMC, a Bluetooth module, 8 LEDs and a fistful of analog buttons. Learn the chip map and the flash memory map before you touch anything. → The device

Understand how it was opened

A voltage-glitch APPROTECT bypass on attempt #8,504 produced the firmware dump that everything else is built on. You almost certainly won't repeat it — but you should know what it bought you. → How it was cracked

Reach the bootloader

Hold Track 1 + Track 4, plug in USB-C. The device enumerates as a serial port for 60 seconds and accepts firmware. This is your no-soldering flash path. → Bootloader & flashing

Speak its protocol

COBS-framed packets, a CRC-8 table, a small opcode palette. Enough to write your own flasher when the web tool is offline. → The wire protocol

Stand up Zephyr

Zephyr RTOS + SDK + the stem_player board. One cmake line produces a flashable zephyr.bin. → Build environment

Build the BSP

ericlewis/sp1-midi is the minimal public, buildable starting point — a MIDI controller, not a stem player, but it gives you working drivers for everything. A complete audio firmware, the tape looper, is also forkable — you'll read it end to end in Ch 13. → The sp1-midi BSP

Add audio

The hard part. The on-disk sample format is a custom byte-interleave, the eMMC is "formatted strangely," and clean-room reference code exists for both. → Audio engine & format

Ship your first firmware

A safe, concrete first project — blink the LEDs from your own code — then the path to a player. → Your first firmware
